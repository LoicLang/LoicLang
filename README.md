# Hi, I'm Loïc 👋

I build applied-AI products around a simple question:

> If AI existed from day one, would we still do it this way?

Most of the time, the answer is no.

That is what I like exploring: how AI can change the way people learn, train, work, decide, and move forward on messy problems.

I'm not obsessed with AI because it is impressive.
I'm interested in AI because it can be a decisive tool when it is used to rethink a real need from the ground up.

A student preparing for an oral exam.
A business team trying to clarify a vague AI request.
A person trying to train consistently despite fatigue and real life.
A company trying to turn messy workflows into something clearer, faster, and more useful.

Different contexts, same question:

**What is the real need, and how do we build a better way to answer it?**

## What I'm building

* **[TaupIA](https://github.com/LoicLang/TaupIA)**
  An AI oral-exam examiner for French *prépa* maths.
  The goal is not to give students the answer, but to challenge their reasoning, ask better questions, and help them progress like a real examiner would.

* **[use-case-assistant](https://github.com/LoicLang/use-case-assistant)**
  An assistant that helps turn a vague business AI idea into a structured, usable intake form.
  It does not just collect answers. It challenges unclear needs, asks for missing context, and helps people frame the right problem before jumping to a solution.

* **FitMAS** *(private)*
  An AI sports coach built around real life, not perfect plans.
  Fatigue, missed sessions, motivation, mental load, progression, setbacks — the things generic training plans usually ignore.

* **MAS** *(private)*
  A lightweight multi-agent system for turning fuzzy business needs into structured project proposals.
  The goal is to help move from "we have an idea" to "we understand the need, the constraints, the options, and the next decision."

## How I think about AI

I don't build AI for AI's sake.

I like the full path:

1. understand the user
2. understand the business context
3. find the real problem behind the request
4. rethink the workflow
5. build a first version
6. test it
7. break it
8. improve it

That loop is what I enjoy.

The model matters.
The product matters more.
The real-world impact matters most.

## Where I come from

I started in industrial automation.

That taught me something that still shapes how I build:

> A technology only matters if it improves the work, the decisions, or the understanding of the people using it.
> Otherwise, it is just a demo.

Automated systems, AI adoption in a bank, tutoring maths, building agents, experimenting with product ideas — the environments are different, but the logic is the same.

Understand the need.
Design the system.
Build.
Test.
Improve.
